What Is An AI-based Recommendation System?

How Does A Recommendation Engine Work?

Typically, a recommendation engine works using AI and machine learning algorithms. Data plays a crucial role in AI-powered recommendation system development. It’s a building block from which you derive patterns and, eventually, make suggestions. The more data you feed it, the more accurate suggestions it makes.

A recommender works in the following steps:

  • Collects Data 

The recommendation engine collects your implicit data. For example, it looks at the products you’ve bought online and the product pages you’ve visited, liked, and searched for. Also, it notices your interaction with the website.

  • Understands Preferences 

AI understands your preferences through explicit data. It tries to understand what you like and dislike. Identifies your behavior and pattern towards different items.

  • Creates User Profiles 

After understanding your choices, the AI-based recommendation engine creates a profile that includes your interests, the categories you browse the most, and the items you are interested in.

  • Look For Similar Users 

AI algorithms look for other users who have similar choices and interests as yours. It then groups individuals with similar interests into a category or cluster.

  • Generates Recommendation

Once AI generates a user profile and categories, it suggests items others in your category have liked. If someone in your group enjoys something, according to the recommender, you might like it too.

  • Make Personalizations

Recommendation engines updates and refine suggestions, making them more accurate over time. The more you interact with a website or service, the more precise suggestions you’ll receive.

  • Feedback Loop

Once you buy something upon recommendation, the recommendation process learns from your actions and improves suggestions further.

Using your online history and past actions, AI algorithms predict what you might need in the future. It makes personalized suggestions that help you explore new things while making your online shopping experience more enjoyable and interesting.

Types of AI-based Recommendation Systems

Based on their underlying algorithms and approaches, different types of recommendation systems include:

  • Collaborative Filtering 

YouTube and Netflix suggest highly personalized content to their users through collaborative filtering.

The basic principle of collaborative filtering is that if two users have the same taste in a product, they might have similar tastes in other products. Firstly, it identifies users who already have shown interest in the same thing, and then based on single user preferences and patterns, it suggests the same thing to other users having a similar pattern.

What’s amazing about this recommendation system is that it helps you find personalized recommendations for other users, even if they aren’t looking for it or haven’t shown interest in the past. It’s a win-win situation for both users!

Moreover, it suggests complex items precisely – without understanding the item itself. Collaborative filtering models are easy to implement and generate more accurate and diverse recommendations.

  • Content-based Recommendation System

It focuses on the things only you are interested in.

Content-based filtering systems suggest similar items you’ve liked or shown interest in the past.. For example, the Music streaming app Spotify suggests personalized playlists, songs, and artists based on the audio features and characteristics of the music you have enjoyed.

Amazon’s product feed is another perfect example of a content-based recommendation system. It recommends products similar to those you’ve previously purchased.

  • Hybrid Recommendation System

As its name suggests, it’s a hybrid system that combines both; a content-based system and collaborative filtering. This system combines the strengths and overcomes the incapabilities of both types to offer you more accurate and diverse suggestions.

The e-commerce giant Amazon’s recommendation system is a perfect example of a hybrid approach. Amazon blends both approaches while offering its users a broad range of recommendations. It suggests items based on your historical preferences while also considering similar users’ preferences.

This hybrid system lets Amazon keep a balance between providing personalized recommendations based on user behavior and the collective intelligence of users. Besides being helpful for users in purchasing products, it improves the platform’s overall engagement and retention.

  • Knowledge-based Recommendation System 

This system gives you recommendations only if you ask for it.

Instead of using users’ behavior data and purchasing patterns, this recommender system makes suggestions based on a user’s specific queries. Let’s break down this with an example to better understand it.

Suppose you have a health concern and you need a help treating it better. You’ll tell your condition and symptoms to the system. The system then looks through its knowledge database and eventually gets you the most suitable treatment or recommendation.

What makes a knowledge-based recommendation system unique from other systems is that it depends on a big pool of knowledge that enables it to make smart suggestions tailored to your needs. For example WebMD is a popular health information website that offers credible and in-depth medical information and news.

Whatever the type is, the ultimate goal of any recommendation system is to keep its user engaged, simplify decision making and eventually increase product demand.

The global data sphere is expanding at a crazy rate. Tons and tons of data types are being generated every second. However, not all data is created equal. Let’s find out how to deal with unstructured data using Artificial Intelligence. But first, have a look at the unstructured data rundown.

What is Unstructured Data?

Unstructured data is wild — it doesn’t fit into traditional rows and columns. Things like text documents, images, videos, social media posts, and even GIFs are unstructured data. All this data is messy and untamed. For this reason, it’s harder to analyze and make the most out of it.

Despite being messy, Unstructured data holds a treasure trove of insights. It could help businesses understand customer sentiments, detect patterns, make predictions, and more if structured properly.

With the right tools and technologies, unstructured data can make sense. For instance, Natural language processing, machine learning, and AI algorithms can sift through it and extract meaning from unstructured data to help you make informed decisions.

Structured Data vs Unstructured Data: What’s the Difference?

Data exists in many forms and sizes but mainly into two types: Structured and unstructured.

Structuring the Data: Why Does it Matter?

Businesses, daily, deal with huge amounts of unstructured data from various sources like customer feedback, reviews, social media comments, in-app reviews, and so on. Gartner estimates that up to 90% of enterprise data is unstructured or semi-structured, and most of it isn’t put to good use.

Moreover, Unstructured data is wider than these feedbacks, numerical values, and facts. In fact, your emotions, opinions, and even thoughts are considered unstructured data.

This data is a goldmine of insights that can impact a business strategy and decision-making. Also, it can make processes better and cut costs. But managing unstructured data can be challenging. It’s time-consuming, expensive, and prone to human bias as well. Plus, it doesn’t offer scalability.

Turning unstructured data into a quantifiable form is quite challenging. It needs more advanced tools, complex rules, and techniques whatsoever.

What Are The Challenges of Managing Unstructured Data?

Managing unstructured data comes with its fair share of challenges. Here are some major challenges you might face.

  • Overwhelming Volume of Data

Unstructured data is exploding at a remarkable rate. According to estimates, the world has created a staggering 175 zettabytes of data up till 2023. That equals 175 trillion gigabytes or 175 million terabytes.

Social media platforms, multimedia content forms, and IoT devices have massively created a sheer volume of unstructured data. Storing, processing, and reviewing such massive amounts of data needs a robust infrastructure and scalable solutions.

  • Tangled Web

Since unstructured data lacks a predefined structure, it’s hard to organize and categorize it effectively. Traditional data processing will not be enough. Also, finding out specific data points within such a massive, tangled web is like searching for a needle in a haystack.

  • Integration Issues

Unstructured data often exist side by side with structured data. Integrating and aligning that unstructured data with existing systems and processes is an uphill task. Careful planning and efficient data mapping must be done to ensure seamless integration and a unified data view.

  • Data Cleansing

One of the major challenges of managing unstructured data is to cleanse and validate it. Almost 60% of a Data Scientist’s effort goes into cleansing the data.

The amount of data you create and deal with daily contains inconsistencies, errors, and noise. It makes the data cleansing process a daunting task. Also, extracting meaningful insights within such data becomes more challenging. So maintaining data quality and overall accuracy takes a lot of work.

  • Requires Computational Resources

Unstructured data like images and text require deep analysis and interpretation to yield insights. Image recognition, natural language processing, and machine learning algorithms play a key role in processing and interpreting data. You need high expertise and computational resources to implement such techniques and algorithms to make the most out of unstructured data.

  • Data Governance and Compliance

Since unstructured data is diverse and falls under the data governance and compliance regulation category, ensuring its adherence to data protection laws, industry standards, and internal policies needs a lot of time.

Unstructured Data Made Easy With Artificial Intelligence 

Unstructured data management is the process of collecting, storing, organizing, and analyzing data with no predefined structure. However, leveraging Artificial Intelligence is the only way to manage such messy, untamed data. The key is to get started.

AI technologies can unlock the potential of unstructured data — Using AI-powered tools, businesses can process, mine, integrate, store, track, index, and report business insights from raw and unstructured data.

  • Automates Data Extraction with NLP 

Whether you want to extract key details from text documents, transcribe audio recordings, or recognize objects in images, AI algorithms can process unstructured data at scale, saving time and effort.

AI-powered techniques such as NLP make computers understand, analyze, and extract meaning from human language. NER (Named Entity Recognition) helps you identify and classify unstructured data such as names, locations, dates, etc. This classification further helps you in analysis and processing.

What’s more, NLP algorithms can automatically generate concise summaries of lengthy text documents. Instead of going through a full-fledged document, you can have a quick overview of the content.

You can also translate text data into multiple languages with NLP. Machine translation models powered by NLP help organizations accurately translate the data enabling them to handle multilingual unstructured data effectively.

  • Text and Sentiment Analysis

What AI algorithms do best is analyze and understand the textual data. Sentiment Analysis, topic modeling, and NER help you derive meaning and context from the unstructured data format. Businesses can get valuable insights into customer sentiments, find out what trends, and make data-driven decisions with unstructured textual data.

Computer vision enables computers to see and understand the world just like us.

This branch of AI can automatically analyze and annotate images and videos. It helps you organize, categorize, and retrieve unstructured visual data smoothly.

In addition to this, computer vision allows for visual search. By comparing visual content and identifying similarities, you can navigate through huge amounts of unstructured data while searching for similar or related images with it.

OCR digitizes unstructured documents, e.g., invoices, receipts, forms, contracts, and handwritten notes, making processing, analyzing, and storing the extracted information easier.
